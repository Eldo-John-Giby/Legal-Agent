## Landmark Precedents for Mock Trial

### ARTICLE 14 (EQUALITY)
ID: PRE_ROYAPPA
CASE: E.P. Royappa v. State of Tamil Nadu (1974)
PRINCIPLE: Equality is a dynamic concept. Arbitrariness is the antithesis of equality. If an action is arbitrary, it is a violation of Art. 14.
APPLICATION: Use this to challenge "unfair" contract enforcement or arbitrary delays.

ID: PRE_AIR_INDIA
CASE: Air India v. Nergesh Meerza (1981)
PRINCIPLE: Classification must be reasonable and have a nexus with the object sought to be achieved.
APPLICATION: Use this to argue that requirements changes (E2) created an unreasonable burden on the Defendant.

### ARTICLE 19 (TRADE/FREEDOM)
ID: PRE_BENNETT_COLEMAN
CASE: Bennett Coleman & Co. v. Union of India (1973)
PRINCIPLE: The "Direct Effect" test. The court looks at the direct effect of the action, not just the intent.
APPLICATION: Argue that while Acme didn't "intend" to stop Smith's business, the *direct effect* of denying access (E4) was an infringement of Art. 19.

ID: PRE_CHINTAMAN_RAO
CASE: Chintaman Rao v. State of M.P. (1950)
PRINCIPLE: Reasonable Restrictions. Restrictions on trade must not be arbitrary or of an excessive nature, beyond what is required in the interest of the public.
APPLICATION: Argue that Acme's original deadline despite scope changes was an "excessive" and "unreasonable" restriction on Smith's ability to work.

### THE GOLDEN TRIANGLE
ID: PRE_MANEKA_GANDHI
CASE: Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978)
PRINCIPLE: Articles 14, 19, and 21 are not silos. They are a "Golden Triangle." Any action must satisfy all three tests of fairness, reasonableness, and non-arbitrariness.
APPLICATION: The foundational argument for why a contract dispute can be viewed through a constitutional lens.
